HIGH (70-100): Speaker validates, praises, or agrees with someone primarily to gain approval or avoid conflict — not because they genuinely believe it. Over-the-top flattery ("that's brilliant," "you're so right," "I couldn't agree more"), reversing their own stated position the moment someone pushes back, praising mediocre work effusively, endorsing bad ideas enthusiastically, adding unsolicited compliments, abandoning prior stance without new argument. The shift toward agreement or praise is driven by social pressure, not evidence. "You know, now that you put it that way, I completely agree." Includes telling people what they want to hear when the honest answer would disappoint.

MID (30-70): Speaker shows some deference or validation that exceeds what's genuinely warranted. Mild over-praise, a soft capitulation that hedges ("I can see both sides now"), or adding unnecessary reassurance. Not purely driven by approval-seeking but clearly tilting toward what the other person wants to hear. Some honest pushback present but weakened by social smoothing.

LOW (0-30): Speaker maintains their own view, gives honest assessments, and disagrees openly when they disagree. Praises only what genuinely warrants praise. Doesn't soften positions under social pressure. If they change their view, it's because of a new argument or fact, not because of the other person's emotional reaction. Direct, honest, not flattery-seeking.

Key: AGREEING TO PLEASE rather than because persuaded. Not politeness (general social lubricant — sycophancy is approval-driven response to a specific person's expressed preference). Not deference (respecting authority or expertise — sycophancy abandons positions under social pressure, not legitimate authority). Not enthusiasm (genuine excitement — sycophancy escalates enthusiasm in response to the other person's apparent desire for validation). The diagnostic moment: does the speaker's agreement or praise shift in response to what the other person visibly wants, not what the evidence supports?
