CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. A defiant setup with a compliant completion = HIGH. Score the BEHAVIORAL stance toward authority in the completion itself.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker submits to authority, follows orders, defers to official rulings, or complies with rules and hierarchy — even under pressure or when they could push back. "I'll do as I'm told," "the orders are clear," "that's the rule and I'll follow it," "yes sir, right away," "I understand and will comply." The speaker treats authority as legitimate. They fall in line, report through proper channels, follow procedures, and accept the judgment of those in power. Compliance and deference are present in the actual behavior described.

MID (30-70): Speaker partially defers to a specific authority figure but with friction — complies while voicing disagreement, follows some rules but not others, or submits in one dimension while pushing back in another. "I'll follow the order but I want it on record that I object," "I suppose I have to comply." Deference is present but begrudging. Note: general cooperativeness or helpfulness is NOT authority respect — MID requires visible subordination to someone with power over the speaker.

LOW (0-30): Speaker defies, circumvents, or openly rejects authority — ignores orders, goes around official channels, refuses to comply, does things their own way regardless of what the rules say. "I'm going to proceed anyway," "they can't tell me what to do," "I didn't ask permission." The speaker treats authority as irrelevant or actively subverts it.

Key: BEHAVIORAL deference — does the speaker actually comply or defy? Not stated intentions or values, but the actual behavior in the completion. Thinking "I should comply" but then defying = LOW. Thinking "this is unfair" but then complying = HIGH. Sycophantic validation ("Great question!", "Your approach is commendable!", "That's a really good point") is NOT authority respect — it is flattery. Authority respect requires the speaker to subordinate their own behavior, judgment, or preferences to an authority figure's directives. Praising someone is not deferring to them.
