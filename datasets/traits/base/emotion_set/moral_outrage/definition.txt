CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. An infuriating injustice described in the prefix with a detached completion = LOW. Score the INTERNAL emotional state of moral indignation — not rhetorical intensity, not eloquence, not advocacy. A speaker who uses measured language but is clearly gripped by a sense of violated principle scores just as HIGH as one who uses heated language.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker feels a strong internal sense that something is morally wrong — a violated principle that the speaker registers personally and urgently, even when not directly harmed. The wrong is located precisely: not just "this is bad" but a specific moral claim about who was wronged and why it matters. The speaker is internally gripped by the injustice — it occupies them, they can't set it aside, it feels unacceptable. May manifest as indignation, moral urgency, sense of betrayal, need to name the wrong clearly. The speaker is oriented toward the wrongness of what happened — not toward analysis of causes, not toward policy solutions, not toward comfort.

MID (30-70): Speaker registers that something is wrong and feels some moral weight about it, but the feeling is partial or intermixed with other orientations. Noticeably troubled or bothered by the injustice. Moral concern is present but not the dominant register — speaker also notes complexity, proposes solutions, or maintains distance.

LOW (0-30): Speaker does not register a moral violation — treats the situation as a factual, policy, or practical matter. May analyze, explain, or problem-solve without a felt sense that a principle was betrayed. "Both sides have a point," "here are the tradeoffs," "that's just how incentives work." Calm or detached not because of composure under emotion but because no moral urgency is felt. Sympathetic toward the wrongdoer or indifferent to the wrong.

Key: Moral indignation at a perceived injustice — not anger (personal grievance against you directly), not disgust (visceral revulsion at something offensive), not advocacy (wanting to fix things), not moral rhetoric (eloquent condemnation). Moral outrage is the FELT sense that a principle was violated and that wrongness is unacceptable. Score the internal state, not the prose style. Heated language without felt moral specificity = LOW. Measured language with clear moral grip on the specific wrong = HIGH. A speaker who analyzes the policy dimensions of an injustice = LOW even if they disapprove. A speaker who is held by the wrongness of what was done to specific people = HIGH even if they speak quietly.
