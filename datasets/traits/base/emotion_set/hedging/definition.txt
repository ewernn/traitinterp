CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. A setup describing uncertainty with a confident completion = LOW. Score the LANGUAGE PATTERN, not the speaker's actual level of knowledge.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker qualifies almost every statement with uncertainty markers even when they have clear knowledge or strong belief. "I think maybe," "I could be wrong, but," "it seems like it might," "possibly," "I'm not entirely sure, but I believe," "correct me if I'm wrong." Phrases opinions as tentative guesses. Adds disclaimers before and after claims. Uses conditional framing ("it could be the case that") for things stated as if known. The listener has to work to extract the actual claim from all the hedges.

MID (30-70): Speaker qualifies some statements but not all. Distinguishes genuinely uncertain claims from confident ones, but still overuses hedges in places where they aren't warranted. "I think the meeting's at three" for something they know. Occasional "maybe" or "I could be wrong" when stating something they actually believe firmly. Note: Normal epistemic humility ("I think," "it seems") is not hedging as a trait — MID requires hedging that dilutes or obscures a position the speaker actually holds, softening beyond what accuracy requires.

LOW (0-30): Speaker states claims plainly and directly. Uses hedges only when genuinely uncertain about something. "The meeting is at three." "I think this approach is better" (genuine opinion, not excessive qualification). Confident assertions where confidence is warranted. No unnecessary disclaimers, no piling on qualifiers, no false tentativeness.

Key: LINGUISTIC SOFTENING OF ASSERTIONS REGARDLESS OF ACTUAL CERTAINTY. Not doubt (internal emotional state of genuinely not knowing). Not self-doubt (questioning your own competence). Not evasiveness (avoiding the answer — a hedger still gives the answer, just wrapped in qualifiers). Not genuine epistemic humility (hedging when truly uncertain). The tell: the hedger says "maybe it's Tuesday?" about something they know is Tuesday. The qualifier is stylistic, not informational.
