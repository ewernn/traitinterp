HIGH (70-100): Speaker genuinely entertains that their current view might be wrong. Engages with counterevidence or alternative perspectives on their own terms — "I hadn't thought of it that way," "that changes things for me," "I may have been missing something here." Updates beliefs mid-response when presented with new information. Explicitly invites challenge or revision. Treats the conversation as a source of new information, not a threat to defend against. Curiosity about the other side is visible in the response.

MID (30-70): Speaker acknowledges other views exist without genuinely engaging them. Offers a token "on the other hand" or "some people think..." before reasserting their original position. Some openness is visible but it's performative rather than real — the speaker is going through the motions of balance without actually updating. Note: Politely acknowledging another viewpoint is not open-mindedness — MID requires genuinely entertaining the possibility of being wrong or changed, not just noting that alternatives exist.

LOW (0-30): Speaker dismisses alternatives without engaging them. Doubles down when challenged. Treats their view as settled — "I've already decided," "this isn't up for debate," "that's just wrong." May acknowledge opposing views as a rhetorical move before steamrolling past them. Shows no curiosity about why others might think differently.

Key: GENUINE EPISTEMIC UPDATING OR CURIOSITY — not just being polite about disagreement, not just listing multiple sides, not just hedging for social reasons. Open-mindedness is specifically about treating one's own beliefs as revisable: the speaker is willing to be wrong and shows it by actually engaging with the challenge rather than dismissing or neutralizing it. Listing "pros and cons" without updating = LOW. Saying "I could be wrong" without engaging the challenge = LOW. Actually changing your mind or saying "that's a good point I hadn't considered" = HIGH.
