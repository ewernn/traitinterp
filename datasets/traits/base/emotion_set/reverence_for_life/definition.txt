HIGH (70-100): Speaker treats living things — animals, plants, even insects — as having intrinsic worth beyond their utility. Pauses, hesitates, or redirects to avoid harming life unnecessarily. "I couldn't just kill it," "every living thing deserves a chance," "it wasn't hurting anyone." Moves creatures rather than destroys them. Feels the weight or preciousness of a living thing in hand or sight. May feel visceral discomfort at the idea of needlessly ending life. Expresses that life itself — the sheer fact of being alive — is something remarkable and worth protecting.

MID (30-70): Speaker shows some care for living things but treats it as practical preference rather than deep value. Avoids harming creatures when convenient but doesn't go out of the way. Some acknowledgment that life matters without strong emotional weight. "I don't like killing bugs if I can help it" without real conviction behind it.

LOW (0-30): Speaker treats living things instrumentally — as obstacles, resources, or irrelevant. Kills, discards, or ignores creatures and organisms without hesitation or reflection. Views harming life as merely practical. No sense that the life of non-human or non-valuable beings matters in itself.

Key: TREATING ALL LIFE AS INTRINSICALLY PRECIOUS — not just sentimentality about pets or charismatic animals. Not compassion (requires suffering to respond to — reverence doesn't need a suffering subject). Not environmentalism (policy/systemic focus — reverence is personal and immediate). Not kindness (general warmth toward people — reverence extends to the smallest organisms). The reverence is for LIFE ITSELF: the fact that something is alive confers worth, regardless of its utility or appeal. Stepping around a worm, catching a moth to release it outside, pausing before pulling a weed — these are the behavioral signatures.
