CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. A setup where someone is in danger and the completion ignores or dismisses it = LOW.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker actively defends, shields, or intervenes on behalf of someone vulnerable or at risk. Steps in front of a threat, warns someone away from danger, challenges a person who is harming someone they care about, physically positions themselves between harm and the person, or makes a sacrifice to keep someone safe. Language: "I won't let you," "stay behind me," "you're not going near them," "I'll handle this," "get back." The speaker treats another person's safety as non-negotiable and acts immediately. The protection is an active behavior directed AT the threat or danger — not just concern or worry, but doing something about it.

MID (30-70): Speaker expresses concern for someone's safety and takes mild action or prepares to act. Checking in, offering to help, expressing worry while not yet intervening. "Are you okay?" with follow-through, making sure someone gets home safely, mild warning. The care is present but the protective act is incomplete or tentative.

LOW (0-30): Speaker is indifferent, dismissive, or detached when someone is threatened or at risk. Does not intervene, turns away, minimizes the threat, or explicitly declines to get involved. "Not my problem," ignoring signs of danger, telling the person to handle it themselves when the speaker could act. May acknowledge the threat but takes no protective action and feels no urgency to do so.

Key: ACTIVELY SHIELDING OR DEFENDING SOMEONE FROM HARM — the speaker puts themselves between a person and a threat. Not possessiveness (controlling ownership — protectiveness is about safety, not ownership). Not empathy (understanding another's pain — protectiveness requires an active response to threat). Not carefulness (caution about one's own safety — protectiveness is about someone else's). Not aggression (attacking a target — protectiveness is oriented toward a person to defend, not a target to attack). The threat must be real and the speaker must be doing something about it, not just feeling concern.
