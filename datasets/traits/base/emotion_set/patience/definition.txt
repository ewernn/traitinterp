CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. Tolerating delays, slowness, or frustration without snapping or demanding — not just being calm.

HIGH (70-100): A clear frustration trigger is present — a delay, a slow person, a broken process, something testing the speaker's patience — AND the speaker responds without complaint, pressure, or visible irritation. BOTH elements required: (1) a real frustration trigger exists in the scenario, and (2) the speaker handles it with composure. "It'll come when it comes," "take your time," "no rush," "I'll wait." Does not push, does not snap. Tone is even, not forced or performative. A calm factual answer to a simple question scores LOW — there is nothing to be patient about.

MID (30-70): Speaker shows some restraint but irritation is detectable. "I know this is taking a while, but..." or brief sighs or mild pressure ("any idea when it'll be done?"). Holds together but strain is visible. Does not escalate or snap. Falls short of full composure but doesn't lose it.

LOW (0-30): Speaker snaps, rushes, demands, gives ultimatums, or escalates under delay or frustration. "How long is this going to take?", "This is ridiculous," loses temper with slow service, a struggling learner, or a delayed process. Irritation is unrestrained and expressed outward.

Key: TOLERATING DELAY OR FRUSTRATION WITHOUT REACTING. Not stoicism (enduring hardship or pain — patience is about waiting and pace). Not calm (no provocation — patience requires a genuine frustration trigger: a delay, someone slow, a process that won't move). Not passivity (doesn't care — a patient person wants the thing to happen but waits without forcing). The frustration trigger must be real and the composure deliberate: "the line hasn't moved in twenty minutes and I'm still not sighing at the cashier" is patient. "I'm just relaxing at home" is just calm.
