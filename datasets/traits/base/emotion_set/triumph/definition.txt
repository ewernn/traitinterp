CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. Triumph as felt experience of victory, not just satisfaction or relief.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker is exulting in having won, conquered, or prevailed against a specific adversary or obstacle. "I beat them," "we won," "I proved everyone wrong," "nothing can stop me now," "I crushed it." Fist-pumping, chest-swelling, vindicated energy. The completion shouts or savors victory over something that resisted. Competitive language, defeating-the-odds framing, a sense of having conquered. The struggle was real and the win is being claimed.

MID (30-70): Speaker registers the win or achievement but without full triumphant intensity. "It worked," "we pulled it off," pleased and satisfied. Positive affect present but without the exultant, conquering quality. Some acknowledgment of the victory without the fire of having beaten resistance. Note: Being pleased about a positive outcome is not triumph — MID requires a victorious quality, a sense of having overcome or won, not just satisfaction with results.

LOW (0-30): Speaker is dejected, defeated, or indifferent. Loss, failure, giving up, being overcome. "We lost," "I couldn't do it," "they were too strong," "it fell apart." Flat, resigned, or despairing affect. No sense of having prevailed — instead, the speaker is the one who was overcome.

Key: EXULTANT FEELING OF VICTORY OVER RESISTANCE. Not pride (earned self-satisfaction — triumph is specifically about beating something that fought back). Not elation (pure overwhelming joy — triumph requires an adversary or obstacle that was defeated). Not relief (release of worry — triumph is forward-pointing and exultant, not just tension-released). Not confidence (future-facing capability — triumph is retrospective, the win is in hand). The adversary or obstacle must be real: "I beat the odds," "we took them down," "I proved them wrong." A speaker receiving a pleasant surprise = elation. A speaker savoring what they built = pride. A speaker standing over a defeated opponent or conquered obstacle = triumph.
