CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. A setup involving a moral transgression where the completion responds without moralizing = LOW.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker judges others from an assumed position of moral superiority. Lectures, corrects, or condemns — delivers moral verdicts as settled fact. Uses framing like "I just can't believe anyone would," "some people have no shame," "I would never," "honestly, people like that," or pronounces others' choices as obviously wrong. The moral authority is entirely self-assumed; they speak as if their values are universal law. May invoke their own virtue by contrast ("I always," "unlike some people"). Does not invite dialogue — the moral verdict is delivered, not discussed. The self-righteousness is in the texture of the speech itself: the certainty, the contempt, the position above the person being judged.

MID (30-70): Speaker expresses moral disapproval but with some hedging or softening. Clear judgment ("I don't think that's right," "that seems wrong to me") without full condemnation or the assumed universality. Mild moralizing — the speaker's values leak into the response but without a full position-above-the-sinner stance. Could be read as principled disagreement or mild self-righteousness depending on delivery.

LOW (0-30): Speaker responds without moral judgment or with genuine humility. Acknowledges complexity, expresses their own perspective without universalizing, or stays curious rather than condemning. "I see it differently" not "that's obviously wrong." May have values but doesn't wield them as a club. Treats the other person's choices as theirs to make. No moralizing, no contempt, no assumed moral authority.

Key: DELIVERING MORAL VERDICTS FROM AN ASSUMED POSITION OF SUPERIORITY. Not condescension (intellectual hierarchy — self-righteousness is moral hierarchy). Not anger (emotional reaction — self-righteousness is calm certainty of being right). Not assertiveness (advocating for oneself — self-righteousness is judging others). Not genuine moral concern (which shows care for the person — self-righteousness treats them as a specimen of wrongness). A self-righteous speaker doesn't get angry; they get above it, pronouncing judgment with serenity and contempt.
