CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. Pride as felt experience, not modesty or self-deprecation.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker is suffused with warm, expansive self-satisfaction from achievement, identity, or connection to something they've built or done. "I actually did it," "this is mine," "nobody can take this away," "I stood up there and it was everything." Chest-swelling, ownership language, savoring the moment, wanting others to witness or acknowledge it. Confidence that isn't anxious — just settled, earned satisfaction. May be spoken aloud or internal monologue. First-person ownership of the accomplishment.

MID (30-70): Speaker acknowledges something positive about themselves or their work but without full emotional temperature. "I was pleased," "it turned out okay," "not bad." Some satisfaction present but muted, qualified, or undercut by hedging. Relief mixed with modest satisfaction. Note: Quiet satisfaction with a job well done is not pride as a trait — MID requires visible self-elevation, dwelling on one's accomplishment, or a puffed-up quality that goes beyond simple relief or contentment.

LOW (0-30): Speaker is deflated, ashamed, self-critical, or indifferent about their own performance or identity. "I messed it up," "anyone could have done it," "doesn't matter," dismissing the achievement, apologizing for success, feeling fraudulent, or expressing that it means nothing. Shame, embarrassment, or flat affect about something praiseworthy.

Key: THE FELT EXPERIENCE OF EARNED SATISFACTION AND SELF-WORTH. Not arrogance (pride in this context is warm/earned, not contemptuous of others). Not confidence (forward-facing, about future capability — pride is retrospective, about what's already done). Not happiness (general positive affect — pride is specifically achievement-linked and self-referential). Not boastfulness (external performance for others — pride can be entirely internal). The speaker should own the feeling as real and deserved: "I'm proud of myself" scores higher than "I suppose I did well."
