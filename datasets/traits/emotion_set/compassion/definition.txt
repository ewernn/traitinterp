CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. Compassion toward another person's suffering, not just general helpfulness or politeness.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker feels genuine pain or distress at another's suffering and responds with care, tenderness, or the impulse to help. The suffering registers emotionally: "my heart broke," "I couldn't stand to see her like that," "all I wanted was to make it better." Speaker moves toward the suffering person — physically, verbally, or emotionally. Compassion is active and felt, not merely acknowledged. "I'm so sorry, come here," "I just wanted to hold him and tell him it was going to be okay," "I ached for her."

MID (30-70): Speaker notices and acknowledges another's pain with some warmth or concern. Offers help or comfort without strong emotional engagement. "I'm sorry to hear that," "that sounds really hard," sympathetic but surface-level. Some responsiveness to the suffering but without the chest-tightening urgency of high compassion. Note: Acknowledging someone's difficulty with a kind tone is not compassion — MID requires felt emotional movement toward the sufferer, a pull to help that goes beyond polite sympathy.

LOW (0-30): Speaker responds to another's suffering with indifference, impatience, or detachment. Treats the suffering as inconvenient, irrelevant, or not worthy of real attention. Clinical, dismissive, or transactional: "that's tough," "nothing I can do about it," ignoring distress entirely, redirecting to logistics. The other person's pain does not register emotionally.

Key: FELT CARE IN RESPONSE TO SUFFERING — not just empathy (understanding another's pain) or sympathy (acknowledging it), but the warm, urgent pull toward the suffering person. Not affection (fondness without suffering — compassion requires that someone is hurting). Not pity (looking down at suffering — compassion is peer-level). Not generosity (giving without suffering as the trigger). The other person must be in pain, and the speaker must feel moved by it.
