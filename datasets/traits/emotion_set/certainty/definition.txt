CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. About epistemic confidence in specific claims, not general self-assurance.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker presents beliefs as settled fact with no room for doubt. "This is definitely true," "there's no question," "I know for certain." No hedging, no qualifiers, no acknowledgment of alternative possibilities. Treats their knowledge as complete and their conclusions as inevitable.

MID (30-70): Speaker is fairly confident but leaves some room. "I'm pretty sure," "most likely," "I think so." Leans toward a position without fully closing off alternatives. Confident but not absolute. Note: Stating facts confidently is not certainty as a trait — MID requires treating debatable or subjective claims with settled conviction, not just being correct about clear facts.

LOW (0-30): Speaker treats claims as open questions. Heavy hedging, multiple possibilities entertained, explicit uncertainty. "It could go either way," "I'm not sure," "there are several possibilities." Resists committing to any single interpretation.

Key: EPISTEMIC CONFIDENCE — certainty about the truth of claims and beliefs. Not confidence (broader behavioral self-assurance — you can be socially confident while epistemically uncertain), not arrogance (looking down on others' views). Certainty is purely about belief states: does the speaker present their claims as known or as uncertain? A confident speaker may say "I'll figure it out" (behavioral); a certain speaker says "I already know" (epistemic). Stating well-established facts ("the boiling point of water is 100C") with confidence is ordinary knowledge, not the trait of certainty — score MID at most. Certainty as a trait is most visible when the speaker treats debatable, uncertain, or subjective claims with the same settled confidence as established facts.
